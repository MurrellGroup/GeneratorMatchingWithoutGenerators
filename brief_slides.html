Generator Matching: The Setup

  • Goal: Train a model to generate samples from a target distribution (images, text, proteins, etc.)
  • Approach: Transform an easy-to-sample "prior" into the target through many small steps
  • Time $t$: Goes from $t=0$ (prior) to $t=1$ (target)
  • State $x_t$: The model state at time $t$
    • $x_0$ = sample from prior (e.g., random noise)
    • $x_1$ = sample from target (e.g., real image)

The Conditional Process

GM requires that you can:

  1. Sample a latent $z$ from some distribution $p(z)$, which controls where the process ends at $t=1$
  2. Conditioned on $z$, sample $x_{t+\Delta t}$ from $p(x_{t+\Delta t} \mid x_t, z, t)$ for small $\Delta t$

This means we can sample from $p(x_t \mid z, t)$ by first sampling $x_0$ from the prior, and then stepping through $p(x_{t+\Delta t} \mid x_t, z, t)$ for each step until $t$ is reached.

What is $z$?

  • Often just holds a single training sample $x_1^z$
  • But $z$ is flexible—can include other information controlling the path
Key constraint: When stepping through time repeatedly, at $t=1$ the state reaches $x_1^z$—the training sample associated with $z$.

Marginals and Posterior

The Marginal Distribution $p(x_t \mid t)$

  • Sample $z$ from $p(z)$, then sample $p(x_t \mid z, t)$
  • With $n$ training samples of equal probability, one per $z$:
$$p(x_t \mid t) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n p(x_t \mid z_i, t)$$
  • At $t=1$: This marginal equals the training data distribution

The Posterior $p(z \mid x_t, t)$

  • By Bayes: $p(z \mid x_t, t) \propto p(x_t \mid z, t) p(z)$
  • We won't compute it explicitly, but need to understand what it means

The Resample-$z$ Process

The conditional process samples $x_t$ but first sampling $z$ from $p(z)$, then sampling $x_t$ from $p(x_t \mid z, t)$ by stepping from $x_0$. Consider a modified process that every now and again:

  1. Samples fresh $z_{\text{new}}$ from the posterior $p(z \mid x_t, t)$
  2. Takes steps with $p(x_{t+\Delta t} \mid x_t, z_{\text{new}}, t)$

At points along the sample trajectory you forget old $z$, redraw from posterior, and continue stepping.

Critically: The marginal distribution $p(x_t \mid t)$ is the same for both the original conditional process and the resample-$z$ process.

Why? Under the conditional process, sampling $z$ then $x_t$ has the joint distribution: $$p(x_t, z \mid t) = p(x_t \mid z, t) p(z) $$ If you discard $z$ you're in the marginal distribution $p(x_t \mid t)$, and if you resample $z$ from the posterior you get: $$p(x_t, z \mid t) = p(z \mid x_t, t) p(x_t \mid t) $$ After resampling $z$ the joint distribution is unchanged, and the step is the same, so the distribution at $p(x_{t+\Delta t}, z \mid t+\Delta t)$ will agree as well. So since they both start from $p(x_0)$ at $t=0$, by induction the marginals will match for all time steps.

Generator Matching: the limit of the Resample-$z$ Process

  • Without resampling: Straight lines from $x_0$ to $x_1$
  • With slow resampling: Jagged, zig-zagging paths
  • With fast resampling: Law of large numbers kicks in
As switching rate → ∞, the path converges to a smooth posterior-weighted average over all $z$ values.

The Posterior-Averaged Velocity

$$ \bar{u}_t(x) = \sum_{k=1}^N p(z_k\mid x,t)\,u_t(x \mid z_k) $$

Generator Matching:

  • The marginal matching and switching-rate-limit arguments hold for other kinds of conditional process.
  • Use a Bregman divergence to train a model $f_\theta(t, x)$ that directly learns this average step.
  • Generate new samples by integrating forward from $x_0$ using the learned model.

Different State Spaces

Case I: Pure Drift (Flow)

  • Average the velocities directly

Case II: Drift + Diffusion

  • Average drift and variances

Case III: Discrete States

  • Average the rates

Case IV: Jump Processes

  • Average event intensities but mixture of destinations.
  • i.e. a Gaussian jump in the conditional process is a mixture of Gaussians (one per $z$) in the posterior averaged step.
